\chapter{Ko--Bathe Plastic-Fracturing Concrete Model}
\label{ch:ko-bathe-concrete}

This chapter documents the implementation of a two-dimensional plastic-fracturing
concrete model following the formulation proposed by Ko and
Bathe~\cite{KoBathe2026} for finite element analysis.  The model combines
three physical mechanisms---\emph{fracturing}, \emph{plasticity}, and
\emph{cracking}---in a strain-driven framework suitable for plane stress
problems.  The implementation is contained in a single header file,
\texttt{KoBatheConcrete.hh}, and satisfies all concept levels of the
\texttt{fall\_n} constitutive interface.

% ========================================================================
\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:kb-theory}

The model belongs to the family of \emph{plastic-fracturing} constitutive
relations for concrete, which recognise that the nonlinear behaviour of
concrete under multi-axial loading arises from three distinct phenomena~\cite{KoBathe2026,BatheRamaswamy1979}:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Fracturing:} progressive degradation of the elastic tangent
        moduli as internal micro-cracking develops under compression.
  \item \textbf{Plasticity:} irrecoverable strains that accumulate once the
        failure envelope is reached.
  \item \textbf{Cracking:} discrete tensile fracture planes (smeared) that
        form when the maximum principal stress exceeds the tensile capacity.
\end{enumerate}

These three mechanisms are coupled through the octahedral stress invariants
and operate in sequence within each strain increment: compressive stiffness
degrades via fracturing; if the resulting stress state violates a yield
surface, plasticity activates; if the local tensile stress exceeds the crack
function, a smeared crack is initiated.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Octahedral Stress Invariants}
\label{ssec:kb-octahedral}

The model uses stress invariants as driving quantities for both
fracturing and cracking.  For a plane stress state
$\bsigma = (\sigma_{xx}, \sigma_{yy}, \tau_{xy})^T$ with $\sigma_{zz} = 0$,
the principal stresses are
\[
  \sigma_1 = \frac{\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy}}{2}
           + \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}{2}\right)^2 + \tau_{xy}^2},
  \qquad
  \sigma_2 = \frac{\sigma_{xx}+\sigma_{yy}}{2}
           - \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy}}{2}\right)^2 + \tau_{xy}^2},
\]
with $\sigma_3 = 0$.  The octahedral normal stress and the deviatoric invariant read
\begin{align}
  \sigma_o &= \frac{\sigma_1 + \sigma_2}{3},
  \label{eq:kb-sigma-o}
  \\[4pt]
  \tau_o   &= \frac{\sqrt{2\,J_2}}{3}
             = \frac{1}{3}\,\sqrt{\sigma_{ij}^{\text{dev}}\,\sigma_{ij}^{\text{dev}}},
  \label{eq:kb-tau-o}
\end{align}
where $J_2 = (\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - \sigma_1\sigma_2)/3$ is the second
deviatoric stress invariant for plane stress ($\sigma_3 = 0$).
The sign convention is engineering: compression gives $\sigma_o < 0$.

\emph{Note:} The paper's $\tau_o$ (Eq.~2b in~\cite{KoBathe2026}) is
$\sqrt{2J_2}/3$, which differs from the classical octahedral shear stress
$\tau_{\text{oct}} = \sqrt{2J_2/3}$ by a factor of $\sqrt{3}$.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Fracturing Mechanism}
\label{ssec:kb-fracturing}

The ascending non-linear branch of the stress--strain curve is captured
entirely by degrading the elastic moduli.  The secant bulk and shear moduli
are defined as \emph{rational} functions (Eqs.~10a--b in~\cite{KoBathe2026}):
\begin{align}
  K_s(\sigma_o) &= \frac{K_e}{1 + A\,(-\sigma_o/f_c')^{\,b-1}},
  \label{eq:kb-ks}
  \\[4pt]
  G_s(\tau_o)   &= \frac{G_e}{1 + c\,(\tau_o/f_c')^{\,d-1}},
  \label{eq:kb-gs}
\end{align}
where $K_e$ and $G_e$ are the initial elastic moduli, $f_c'$ is the
uniaxial compressive strength, and $A, b, c, d$ are empirically fitted
coefficients depending on $f_c'$ (see Section~\ref{ssec:kb-parameters}).
The formula~\eqref{eq:kb-ks} applies for $-\sigma_o/f_c' \le 2$; an
extended branch is defined for higher confinement.

The fracture history is driven by the \emph{maximum} octahedral invariants
ever reached:
\[
  \bar\sigma_o = \min_{t'\le t} \sigma_o(t'),
  \qquad
  \bar\tau_o   = \max_{t'\le t} \tau_o(t').
\]
During unloading $\sigma_o > \bar\sigma_o$, the moduli remain frozen at
their last-degraded values, representing the irreversibility of
micro-cracking.

The tangent moduli follow from differentiation (Eqs.~11a--b):
\begin{align}
  K_c &= \frac{K_e}{1 + A\,b\,(-\bar\sigma_o/f_c')^{\,b-1}},
  \label{eq:kb-kc}
  \\[4pt]
  G_c &= \frac{G_e}{1 + c\,d\,(\bar\tau_o/f_c')^{\,d-1}}.
  \label{eq:kb-gc}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Confining pressure $\sigma_{\text{id}}$.}
A confining pressure correction is introduced (Eq.~10b in the paper):
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\text{id}} = \frac{k\,f_c'\,(\tau_o/f_c')^{n}}{1 + l\,(-\sigma_o/f_c')^{m}},
  \label{eq:kb-sigma-id}
\end{equation}
which is subtracted from the volumetric stress contribution:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{ij} = 2\,G_s\,e^{\text{dev}}_{ij} + K_s\,e_{kk}\,\delta_{ij} - \sigma_{\text{id}}\,\delta_{ij}.
  \label{eq:kb-stress-with-sigma-id}
\end{equation}

Given $K$ and $G$, the plane stress constitutive matrix is
\[
  \mathbf{C}^{\text{ps}} = \frac{E}{1-\nu^2}
  \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & \nu & 0 \\ \nu & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \tfrac{1-\nu}{2}
  \end{bmatrix},
  \qquad
  E = \frac{9KG}{3K+G},
  \quad
  \nu = \frac{3K-2G}{2(3K+G)}.
\]

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Compression Coordinate System}
\label{ssec:kb-compression-coords}

Plasticity yield surfaces are defined not in principal-stress space but in a
\emph{compression coordinate} system.  Given the principal elastic strains
$(\varepsilon_1 \ge \varepsilon_2)$, define the compressive magnitudes (positive in
compression)
\[
  D_1 = \max(-\varepsilon_1,\, 0), \qquad D_2 = \max(-\varepsilon_2,\, 0),
\]
and the compression coordinates
\begin{align}
  \tilde e_1 &= D_2 - D_1 \ge 0 \qquad \text{(deviatoric compression)},
  \label{eq:kb-e1}
  \\[4pt]
  \tilde e_2 &= D_1 + D_2 \ge 0 \qquad \text{(volumetric compression)}.
  \label{eq:kb-e2}
\end{align}
For the plasticity yield functions, these are mapped to a set of three
elastic coordinates:
\[
  \hat{e}_1 = \frac{|\tilde e_2|}{\sqrt{3}}, \qquad
  \hat{e}_2 = \tilde e_1, \qquad
  \hat{e}_3 = \frac{\tilde e_2}{2},
\]
corresponding to hydrostatic, uniaxial, and biaxial loading paths
respectively.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Plasticity Yield Surfaces}
\label{ssec:kb-plasticity}

Three yield functions are defined in the compression coordinate space
(Eq.~15c--d in~\cite{KoBathe2026}):
\begin{align}
  f_1 &= |\hat{e}_1| - f_c'\,f_{p1}(\hat{e}_{p1}) \le 0, \label{eq:kb-f1} \\
  f_2 &= \hat{e}_2   - f_c'\,f_{p2}(\hat{e}_{p2}) \le 0, \label{eq:kb-f2} \\
  f_3 &= \hat{e}_3   - f_c'\,f_{p3}(\hat{e}_{p3}) \le 0, \label{eq:kb-f3}
\end{align}
where $\hat{e}_{pj}$ are effective plastic strains accumulated per surface
(Eq.~15b).  The failure curves (Eq.~15d) are:
\begin{align}
  f_{p1}(\hat{e}_{p1}) &= c_{p1}\left(1 + \frac{\hat{e}_{p1}-a}{a}\right)^{-s}\hat{e}_{p1},
  \label{eq:kb-fp1} \\
  f_{p2}(\hat{e}_{p2}) &= c_{p2}\exp\!\left[-\left(\frac{\hat{e}_{p2}}{c_2}\right)^{m_2}\right]\hat{e}_{p2},
  \label{eq:kb-fp2} \\
  f_{p3}(\hat{e}_{p3}) &= c_{p3}\exp\!\left[-\left(\frac{\hat{e}_{p3}}{c_3}\right)^{m_3}\right]\hat{e}_{p3},
  \label{eq:kb-fp3}
\end{align}
with fixed constants $c_{p1}=3$, $c_{p2}=36$, $c_{p3}=12$ and hydrostatic
parameters $a = 2.5\times 10^{-5}$, $s = 0.8$.

The Appendix~C coefficients $m_2, c_2, m_3, c_3$ are coupling parameters
that depend on the hydrostatic effective plastic strain $\hat{e}_{p1}$ 
(Eqs.~C.1--C.2 in the paper):
\begin{align}
  m_2 &= \frac{1}{\ln\!\left(\sqrt{2/3}\,\alpha_2 / (\beta_2\,\hat{\varepsilon}_{p2})\right)},
    &  c_2 &= (\beta_2\,\hat{\varepsilon}_{p2})\,m_2^{1/m_2}, \\
  \alpha_2 &= 1 + 0.575\,(e_{cp2})^{0.315}, &
  \beta_2  &= 1 + 8.4\,(e_{cp2})^{0.25}, \\
  e_{cp2}  &= \max(0,\; K_e\,\hat{e}_{p1} - 0.45), &
  e_{cp3}  &= \max(0,\; K_e\,\hat{e}_{p1} - 2.17),
\end{align}
with reference plastic strains $\hat{\varepsilon}_{p2} = 0.0012$ and
$\hat{\varepsilon}_{p3} = 0.0025$.

The return mapping uses a Newton iteration per active surface
(Eqs.~27--29):  for each $f_j > 0$, solve
\[
  f_j = \hat{e}_j - \Delta\lambda_j - f_c'\,f_{pj}(\hat{e}_{pj} + \sqrt{2/3}\,\Delta\lambda_j) = 0
\]
for $\Delta\lambda_j \ge 0$ using
\[
  \Delta\lambda_j^{(I+1)} = \Delta\lambda_j^{(I)} - \frac{f_j^{(I)}}{-1 - \sqrt{2/3}\,\partial(f_c' f_{pj})/\partial\hat{e}_{pj}}.
\]

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Cracking Mechanism}
\label{ssec:kb-cracking}

Tensile cracking is governed by a Menetr\'ey--Willam crack function in the
octahedral stress plane (Eq.~16 in~\cite{KoBathe2026}):
\begin{equation}
  g(\sigma_o, \tau_o) = \tau_o - \frac{0.944\,f_c'}{w(e)}\,
    \left(t_p - \frac{\sigma_o}{f_c'}\right)^{0.724},
  \label{eq:kb-crack-fn}
\end{equation}
where $t_p = f_t / f_c'$ is the tensile-to-compressive strength ratio, and
$w(e)$ is an elliptic function of the eccentricity parameter:
\begin{align}
  w(e) &= \frac{4(1-e^2)\cos^2\theta + (2e-1)^2}
               {2(1-e^2)\cos\theta + (2e-1)\sqrt{4(1-e^2)\cos^2\theta + 5e^2-4e}},
  \label{eq:kb-w-eccentricity} \\
  e    &= 0.670551\,(t_p - \sigma_o/f_c')^{0.133}.
  \label{eq:kb-eccentricity}
\end{align}
A crack initiates when $g > 0$ \emph{and} the maximum principal stress is
tensile.

The model tracks up to two orthogonal smeared cracks in each material point.
The second crack, when formed, is forced to be perpendicular to the first.
Each crack possesses:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a fixed normal direction $\mathbf{n}$,
  \item an opening strain $\varepsilon_{nn}$ (projected normal strain),
  \item a maximum historical opening strain $\bar\varepsilon_{nn}$,
  \item a closing flag (re-contact when $\varepsilon_{nn} < 0$).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Cracked tangent (Eq.~21).}
After a crack forms, the tangent matrix is modified in the crack-local
coordinate system with stiffness retention parameters:
\begin{align}
  E_{nn} &= \eta_N\,\lambda_a & &\text{(normal, } \eta_N = 0.0001\text{)}, \label{eq:kb-eta-N} \\
  G_{ns} &= \eta_S\,G_c       & &\text{(shear, }  \eta_S = 0.1\text{)}.    \label{eq:kb-eta-S}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Tension softening (Eqs.~22--23).}
For the softening branch between peak and ultimate strains:
\begin{align}
  \varepsilon_{tp} &= \frac{f_c'\,t_p}{K_e + \tfrac{4}{3}\,G_e},
  \label{eq:kb-eps-tp} \\
  \varepsilon_{tu} &= \frac{2\,G_f}{f_c'\,t_p\,l_b},
  \label{eq:kb-eps-tu} \\
  C_{ts} &= -\frac{f_t}{\varepsilon_{tp} - \varepsilon_{tu}},
  \label{eq:kb-Cts}
\end{align}
where $G_f$ is the fracture energy (N/mm) and $l_b$ is the characteristic
element length (mm).  When $\varepsilon_{tp} \le \varepsilon_{nn} \le
\varepsilon_{tu}$, the normal stiffness $E_{nn}$ is replaced by $C_{ts}$.
When the crack closes ($\varepsilon_{nn}<0$), full compressive normal
stiffness is restored (\emph{re-contact}).

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Material Parameters}
\label{ssec:kb-parameters}

All coefficients are derived from a single primary parameter: the uniaxial
compressive strength $f_c'$ (in~MPa, positive).

\paragraph{Elastic moduli (Eq.~A.1).}
\begin{equation}
  K_e = 11000 + 3.2\,f_c' \;\text{(MPa)},
  \qquad
  G_e = 9224 + 136\,f_c' + 3296\!\times\!10^{-15}\,(f_c')^{8.273} \;\text{(MPa)}.
  \label{eq:kb-elastic-params}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Fracture degradation (Eqs.~A.2--A.4).}
For $f_c' \le 31.7$~MPa:
\begin{equation}
  A = 0.516,\quad
  c = 3.573,\quad
  d = 2.12 + 0.0183\,f_c',\quad
  m = -2.415,\quad
  n = 1.0.
  \label{eq:kb-fracture-coeffs-low}
\end{equation}
For all $f_c'$:
\begin{equation}
  b = 2 + 1.81\!\times\!10^{-8}\,(f_c')^{4.461},\quad
  k = \frac{4}{1 + 1.087\,(\max(f_c'-15, 0.1))^{0.23}},\quad
  l = 0.222 + 0.01086\,f_c' - 0.000122\,(f_c')^2.
  \label{eq:kb-fracture-coeffs-all}
\end{equation}
For $f_c' > 31.7$~MPa, $A$, $c$, $d$, $m$, $n$ have extended formulas
(see Appendix~A of~\cite{KoBathe2026}).

\paragraph{Tension.}
The tensile strength ratio follows the fib~Model~Code approximation
\begin{equation}
  t_p = 0.3\,(f_c')^{-1/3},
  \quad\text{clamped to}\quad
  t_p \in [0.05,\, 0.10].
  \label{eq:kb-tp}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Softening and crack retention.}
The fracture energy is taken as $G_f = 0.06\;\text{N/mm}$ (default), the
element length as $l_b = 100\;\text{mm}$ (default), and the stiffness
retention factors as $\eta_N = 0.0001$, $\eta_S = 0.1$.

\section{Implementation Architecture}
\label{sec:kb-architecture}

The implementation resides in a single header:
\begin{center}
\texttt{src/materials/constitutive\_models/non\_lineal/KoBatheConcrete.hh}
\end{center}
and consists of three C++ types.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{KoBatheParameters}
\label{ssec:kb-params-class}

A plain aggregate containing all material coefficients derived from $f_c'$
at construction time.  Includes the elastic moduli, fracture degradation
coefficients, confining pressure parameters, tension parameters, and
plasticity failure curve constants.

\begin{lstlisting}
struct KoBatheParameters {
    double fc, Ke, Ge, Ee, nue;
    double A_coeff, b_coeff, c_coeff, d_coeff;
    double k_coeff, l_coeff, m_coeff, n_coeff;
    double tp, Gf, lb;
    static constexpr double cp1=3, cp2=36, cp3=12;
    static constexpr double eps_p2_ref=0.0012, eps_p3_ref=0.0025;
    static constexpr double hydro_a=2.5e-5, hydro_s=0.8;
    static constexpr double eta_N=0.0001, eta_S=0.1;

    explicit KoBatheParameters(double fc_MPa,
        double tp_ratio=0.0, double Gf_Nmm=0.06, double lb_mm=100.0);
};
\end{lstlisting}

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{KoBatheState}
\label{ssec:kb-state-class}

The internal history variable set tracks:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fracture history:} maximum octahedral invariants
        $\bar\sigma_o$ and $\bar\tau_o$.
  \item \textbf{Plastic strain:} $\bvarepsilon^p$ in Voigt format
        $(\varepsilon_{xx}^p, \varepsilon_{yy}^p, \gamma_{xy}^p)$.
  \item \textbf{Effective plastic strains:} $\hat{e}_{p1}, \hat{e}_{p2},
        \hat{e}_{p3}$ per yield surface.
  \item \textbf{Crack state:} number of cracks (0, 1, or 2), normal
        directions, opening strains, and closure flags.
  \item \textbf{Committed total strain:} for state tracking.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The state provides accessors \texttt{eps\_p()} and
\texttt{eps\_bar\_p()} for integration with the library's
\texttt{InternalFieldSnapshot} post-processing system.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{KoBatheConcrete}
\label{ssec:kb-main-class}

The main constitutive class satisfying the \texttt{fall\_n} concept hierarchy:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Level 1:} \texttt{ConstitutiveRelation}
    --- \texttt{compute\_response(k, alpha)}, \texttt{tangent(k, alpha)},
        \texttt{commit(alpha, k)}.
  \item \textbf{Level 2b:} \texttt{InelasticConstitutiveRelation}
    --- \texttt{compute\_response(k)}, \texttt{tangent(k)}, \texttt{update(k)},
        \texttt{internal\_state()}.
  \item \textbf{Level 3:} Combines Level~1 and Level~2b.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Algorithm Flow}
\label{ssec:kb-algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:kb-evaluate} summarises the evaluation procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[htb]
\SetAlgoLined
\SetKwInOut{Input}{Input}
\SetKwInOut{Output}{Output}
\Input{Total strain $\bvarepsilon$, previous state $\alpha_n$}
\Output{Stress $\bsigma$, tangent $\mathbf{C}$, updated state $\alpha_{n+1}$}
$\bvarepsilon^e \leftarrow \bvarepsilon - \bvarepsilon^p_n$ \tcp*{Elastic strain}
$(K_s, G_s, K_c, G_c, \sigma_{\text{id}}) \leftarrow$ fracture moduli from $(\bar\sigma_{o,n}, \bar\tau_{o,n})$\;
$\bsigma \leftarrow \mathbf{C}^{\text{ps}}(K_s, G_s)\,\bvarepsilon^e - \sigma_{\text{id}}\,\boldsymbol{\delta}$ \tcp*{Eq.~\eqref{eq:kb-stress-with-sigma-id}}
$(\sigma_o, \tau_o) \leftarrow$ invariants of $\bsigma$ \tcp*{Eqs.~\eqref{eq:kb-sigma-o}--\eqref{eq:kb-tau-o}}
Update history: $\bar\sigma_o \leftarrow \min(\bar\sigma_{o,n},\sigma_o)$,
  $\bar\tau_o \leftarrow \max(\bar\tau_{o,n},\tau_o)$\;
Recompute moduli and stress with updated history\;
$\mathbf{C}_{\text{tan}} \leftarrow \mathbf{C}^{\text{ps}}(K_c, G_c)$\;
\If{max principal stress $> 0$ \textbf{and} $g(\sigma_o,\tau_o) > 0$}{
  Initiate new crack with normal along max principal direction\;
}
Update crack opening strains, closure flags\;
Apply cracking to $\bsigma$ and $\mathbf{C}_{\text{tan}}$ via $\eta_N, \eta_S$\;
$(\hat{e}_1,\hat{e}_2,\hat{e}_3) \leftarrow$ compression coordinates\;
\If{any $f_j > 0$ (yield violated)}{
  Newton return mapping per surface $\rightarrow$ update $\bvarepsilon^p, \hat{e}_{pj}$\;
  $\bvarepsilon^e \leftarrow \bvarepsilon - \bvarepsilon^p$\;
  $\bsigma \leftarrow \mathbf{C}_{\text{sec}}\,\bvarepsilon^e - \sigma_{\text{id}}\,\boldsymbol{\delta}$\;
}
\Return $(\bsigma, \mathbf{C}_{\text{tan}}, \alpha_{n+1})$\;
\caption{Ko--Bathe constitutive evaluation.}
\label{alg:kb-evaluate}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Oriented fracture planes.}
The crack normal vectors \texttt{qp\_crack\_normal\_1} and
\texttt{qp\_crack\_normal\_2} are stored as 3-component vectors
($n_x, n_y, 0$).  In ParaView, these can be displayed using the
\emph{Glyph} filter:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Load the Gauss cloud \texttt{.vtu} file.
  \item Apply the Glyph filter with Glyph Type = Disc or Arrow.
  \item Set ``Orientation'' to \texttt{qp\_crack\_normal\_1}.
  \item Set ``Scale'' to \texttt{qp\_num\_cracks} or a constant.
  \item Set ``Masking Mode'' to threshold $\ge 1$ on
        \texttt{qp\_num\_cracks} to show only cracked points.
\end{enumerate}
This produces a visualization of oriented fracture planes at each
integration point where cracks have formed.

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
The snapshot path uses compile-time detection:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{KoBatheState::eps\_p()} $\to$ \texttt{plastic\_strain}
  \item \texttt{KoBatheState::eps\_bar\_p()} $\to$ \texttt{equivalent\_plastic\_strain}
  \item \texttt{KoBatheState::num\_cracks} $\to$ \texttt{InternalFieldSnapshot::num\_cracks}
  \item \texttt{KoBatheState::crack\_normals} $\to$ promoted to 3D vectors
  \item \texttt{KoBatheState::sigma\_o\_max}, \texttt{tau\_o\_max} $\to$ fracture history scalars
\end{itemize}

\section{Concept Satisfaction}
\label{sec:kb-concepts}

The implementation satisfies the three-level concept hierarchy at compile
time, verified via \texttt{static\_assert}:

\section{Design Decisions}
\label{sec:kb-design-notes}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Single header.}  Consistent with the library's
        header-only philosophy.

  \item \textbf{Rational vs.\ exponential moduli.}  The paper's
        formulation~\eqref{eq:kb-ks}--\eqref{eq:kb-gs} uses rational
        degradation functions (not exponential), giving a better match to
        the experimental ascending branch.

  \item \textbf{Secant--tangent distinction.}  Secant moduli ($K_s, G_s$)
        for stress; tangent moduli ($K_c, G_c$) for the algorithmic
        tangent matrix.

  \item \textbf{Confining pressure $\sigma_{\text{id}}$.}  The stress
        includes a confining pressure correction (Eq.~\eqref{eq:kb-sigma-id})
        that accounts for triaxial effects even in the plane stress setting.

  \item \textbf{Plane stress only.}  Extension to 3D requires
        generalising the compression coordinates, yield surfaces, and
        crack normal tracking to three orthogonal planes.

  \item \textbf{VTK integration.}  The snapshot-based export provides
        crack normal vectors as 3D fields for direct ParaView Glyph rendering of
        oriented fracture planes at integration points.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Possible Extensions}
\label{sec:kb-extensions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Full 3D generalisation}: extend compression coordinates
        to all three principal directions; add a third crack plane.
  \item \textbf{Consistent tangent for plasticity}: derive the full
        algorithmic tangent including the plastic correction.
  \item \textbf{Rate effects}: temperature and strain-rate dependence through
        modification of the fracture degradation functions.
  \item \textbf{Mesh-adjusted softening}: use actual element sizes from
        the mesh for the characteristic length $l_b$.
\end{itemize}
